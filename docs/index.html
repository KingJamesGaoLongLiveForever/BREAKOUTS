Donchian Channel Breakout

A transparent long/short breakout strategy on liquid US equities & ETFs, validated with rolling walk-forward optimization.

Parameters searched in-sample (1y train) → applied out-of-sample (1y test). Risk-free rate: 4.00% · Timeout: 20 trading days · Stop: 2.0× ATR(20) · Profit target: 3.0× ATR(20) · Cost: 2.0 bps / side

1. Strategy in one paragraph

This strategy takes long positions when price breaks out above its prior N-day high and short positions when it breaks below its prior N-day low — the classic Donchian channel rule used by the 1980s Turtle Traders. A breakout is read as statistical evidence that a trend has begun. Every trade is protected by a volatility-scaled stop at entry ± 2.0 × ATR(20), aims for a symmetric volatility-scaled target at ± 3.0 × ATR(20), and is closed at the market after a 20-day hard timeout if neither boundary is reached. Position size is set so that a stop-out costs roughly 0.2% of current equity, capped at 5% of equity per name. The N we use for the entry channel is not fixed — it is re-chosen each year by an in-sample Sharpe search, then frozen for the next year of live trading. Everything in this page is derived from the out-of-sample window only.

2. Asset selection

I screened a universe of 56 liquid large-cap US stocks and broad-market / sector ETFs chosen to span mega-cap tech, financials, energy, healthcare, staples, and industrial names plus high-volume thematic ETFs (SPY, QQQ, GLD, USO, XLE, XLK, …). The universe was intentionally concentrated in names with deep liquidity and tight spreads so that the 2 bps/side cost assumption is defensible. Across the walk-forward OOS period the most active breakout names were: GLD (64), EEM (62), BAC (61), CAT (60), SLV (57). The featured price chart below uses <strong>GLD</strong> because it produced the largest number of well-separated, clean channel breaks — making the strategy's behavior visually easy to follow.

3. Breakout definition & parameters

A long breakout fires on bar t when close(t) > max(high(t-N), …, high(t-1)). A short breakout is the symmetric case on the low. Here N (the entry channel length) is the single parameter that walk-forward optimization tunes; we search the grid [20, 40, 55, 100]. Signals formed on the close execute at the next bar's open, never the same close, so there is no look-ahead. A position is only opened if ATR(20) is at least 0.50% of price — filtering out dead markets where a "break" is really noise. A trade then exits via the first of:

  1. Stop-loss: intrabar low (for longs) or high (for shorts) pierces entry ∓ 2.0 × ATR(20) → filled at the stop level.
  2. Successful trade: intrabar high (for longs) or low (for shorts) reaches entry ± 3.0 × ATR(20) → filled at the target level.
  3. Timeout: the position has been held for 20 bars and neither of the above has fired → closed at that bar's close (market-on-close).

A one-paragraph plain-English version of the detector function lives in the docstring of src/quant_research/breakout/signals.py (detect_breakouts); every threshold above is a named constant in src/quant_research/breakout/config.py.

10. Assumptions

  • Daily bars, split- and dividend-adjusted (yfinance, auto_adjust=True).
  • Signals formed on the close; execution on the next bar's open.
  • Round-trip cost: 4.0 bps of notional (commission + spread + slippage), applied on both entry and exit.
  • Single shared capital pool of $1,000,000. Every trade, on every ticker, is sized off the same running equity. Exits are processed before new entries on each bar, so capital released by a closing trade can be reused that same day. A hard 1.0× gross leverage cap (|long|+|short| ÷ equity) prevents concurrent entries from implicitly over-committing the book.
  • Risk-free rate: 4.00% per annum, converted to a daily rate for Sharpe / Sortino. The reported equity curve is pure strategy — idle cash is not accreted at rf, so Sharpe can subtract rf explicitly without any double-counting.
  • Walk-forward signals are computed on the full price history and then sliced to each test window, so rolling-window lookbacks warm up before the window starts (no NaN signals at block boundaries).
  • Only one open position per ticker at a time; no pyramiding.
  • Stop-loss and profit-target priority: if both conditions fire on the same bar, the stop-loss is assumed to hit first (conservative fill).
